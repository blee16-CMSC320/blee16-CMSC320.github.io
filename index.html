Video Game Sales Dataset Analysis Tutorial¶

Background¶

Video games are growing in popularity throughout the world. As access to computers and technology grows around the globe, so does the video game market, making it a billion dollar industry. However, modern titles are often complex pieces of software and can be very expensive and time consuming to produce, making them risky investments. Developing the wrong game for the wrong audience can lead to poor sales, which can prove disasterous in such a volatile industry.

Game publishers, such as EA (Electronic Arts), Nintendo, and Activision fund the development of games, often through third party developers, or in-house teams. Such publishers will often invest hundreds of millions of dollars into development, often paying millions more for marketing.

It is therefore important for publishers and developers to understand their target demographics as different game genres may be popular in different regions, and different publishers may be better known in some regions over others. By analyzing video game sales data, we can curate information can allow publishers to make decisions on what kinds of games to fund, improve their marketing strategies, and motivate the inclusion of additional features, such as language support. Such knowledge would be of high value to developers and publishers and is important to the future of the industry.

The Booming Game Industry: https://www.businessinsider.com/video-game-industry-120-billion-future-innovation-2019-9
Risks of AAA development: https://medium.com/@GWBycer/the-risks-of-aaa-game-development-15508c970a78

Data¶

We will be operating on a dataset containing information about 11493 titles. The data is represented by a table, which each row representing a video game title, with columns that include information about the title. A link to the dataset can be found here: https://www.kaggle.com/gregorut/videogamesales/data

Each table row contains the following information about each title.

  • Rank (By Global_Sales)
  • Name
  • Platform (Consoles only)
  • Year
  • Genre
  • Publisher
  • NA_Sales (North America)
  • EU_Sales (Europe)
  • JP_Sales (Japan)
  • Other_Sales
  • Global_Sales

Removing Unwanted Rows and Columns¶

Datasets also frequently have missing data values. To make our lives easier, we'll drop all incomplete data. We also often work with data that is old and not as relevant to our studies. We'll also remove games that have 0 recorded sales. In addition since we'll mostly be focusing on contemporary video games so we'll exclude games that were released before 1994, the year Sony's Playstation launched. Because of some missing values for more recent titles, we'll also only be looking at games made in 2016 or earlier. We'll also remove Wii Sports due to the fact that it's sales were highly boosted because of it's inclusion with the Nintendo Wii, making it a unique outlier.

Less of an issue is when we have unnecessary columns. We don't really need the "Rank" column, so we can go ahead and drop that.

Hypothesis Testing with Multiple Linear Regression¶

What is Linear Regression¶

Linear regression is the process of fitting a linear equation to a set of data that may or may not have a linear relationship between the independent and dependent variable. In other terms, if one were to visualize data on a scatterplot, performing a linear regression would be akin to the process of calculating and graphing a line that best 'fits' the data.

Info about linear regression and its different types: https://www.statisticssolutions.com/what-is-linear-regression/

Applying Linear Regression to Our Data¶

When developing a video game, publishers must make a decision on whether to develop the game for the current generation of consoles, or to wait to release on the next generation. To help answer this question, We'll be using linear regression to examine how the age of a game console affects the sales of a game released for it. To focus our results, We will be looking at games published by Nintendo. We will also exclude PC games, as well as as games released for consoles before 1994.

Our Hypothesis¶

A hypothesis is defined as a proposed explanation for a phenomenon. Our hypothesis is: As the age of a game console increases, the number of units sold will decrease. Our null hypothesis is that there is no relationship between the age of a console and the number of units sold. We can reject our null hypothesis if there is no statistical significance found between the age of a console, and the number of sales.

Interpreting OLS Regression Results¶

Now we have some good info. However the ones we're interested in are R-Squared, coef and P value.

R-Squared tells us what percent of variance is explained by our model. Since our value is 0.038, that means our model doesn't account for the high amount of variance. Examining the scatterplot shows that there are many outliers from the line fit that our model isn't very happy with.

Now we take a look at the table under the first section of the OLS summary. On the left, we see the intercept, and stats about each platform and the platform age affect sales.

Coef essentially tells us the slope of our fitted linear function. According to our regression analysis, for every year old that a console is, sales decrease by .1923 million units sold.

P value (P>|t|) tells us whether our findings are statistically significant. A p-value of lower than 0.05 usually indicates statistic significance. This would mean that Platform_Age, as well as the and DS and Wii are statistically significant. Because our Platform_Age is statistically significant, we can reject the null hypothesis.

Machine Learning Analysis¶

In this next section, we will use random forest regression to predict the sales of a game based off of it's attributes. Random Forest Model¶

A random forest is a machine learning algorithm that uses results from multiple decision trees to make predictions based off of training data. A decision tree is a flow-chart type tree structure in which each node makes a decision based off the given attributes. However, decision trees are vulnerable to overfitting, meaning that decision trees are very good at making a model that only works well with the data it was trained on. A random forest combines the results from multiple decision trees to create a more accurate model.

Interpreting Results¶

Mean Absolute Error is the average magnitude of the difference between our predicted and actual values. Our mean absolute error is 0.283, which means that our sales predictions are off by an average of 280,000 units sold.

Mean Squared Error is the average of the squared magnitude of difference between our predicted and actual values.

Root Mean Squared Error is the root of the Mean Squared Error. The RMSE is similar to the Mean Absolute Error, but because the difference is squared before being averaged, this metric gives more weight to high deviations when telling us how off our model is. Our RMSE is 840,000 units, which indicates that our model had a number of errors of high magnitude.
